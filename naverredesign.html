Naver Dictionary

Naver Dictionary is an online dictionary created by Naver. This app is often recommended as the “best” Korean - English Dictionary for Korean learners. The app version of Naver Dictionary offers many features targeted towards Korean learners, such as “Word of the Day”, “Conversation of the Day”, and Test of Proficiency in Korean (TOPIK) vocabulary lists.

Problems

The current design of the Naver Dicitonary application has issues including cluttered UI making it difficult for users to successfully use the core funtion of the app, unstructured and overwhelming search results. Poor visual hierarchy.

Scope of Project

This case study focuses on redesigning the landing page, word search page, and the settings menu options. I chose to focus this project on those pages as these are the most high impact screens. For a user to sucessfully search a word definition they will always need to use the landing page to search and find the definition on the word search page. I decided to also include the settings menu in this redesign as many negative feedback reflects that after a update users are having a difficult time finding the proper dictionary for their use cases which is causing them to abandon the app out of frustration. And so the goals of this redesign are as follows:

  • make the landing page visually cleaner and focused on the primary task of searching a word’s definition
  • reorganize the word search results page to prioritize relevance
  • create a streamlined method to change the home dicitonary and other settings
  • build trust with the users by creating an interface that align with their expectations

Target Users

Naver dicitionary offers many many many different dicitonaries in their apps. I cannot feasibly redesign every page that they current have. And so I limit the scope to the following:

  • English-speaking learns of Korean all levels
  • English speakers living or traveling in Korea who rely on the app for real-time searches

Design Principles

This redesign is grounded in Jakob Nielsen’s 10 Usability Heuristics:

  • Aesthetic and Minamlist Design
    • Interfaces should not contain irrelevant or rarely needed information. Every extra unit of information competes with the relevant content and diminishes visibility.
  • Match Between System and the Real World
    • The system should speak the users' language e.g. using familiar words, phrases, and concepts. For example, showing “책” as the result for “book” instead of obscure technical terms or the English word written using Hangul (북).
  • User Control and Freedom
    • Users should be able to confidently navigate the the app settings and understand what is going on
